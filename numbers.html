Numbers, not names

April 2026

It's strange how there's war going on, and somehow the most affecting thing to us is the shortage of LPG cylinders. Each day begins with news—TV reports, newspapers, casual talk—everything includes the ongoing conflict. But what do we do? Make jokes about it, or call sensitive people 'too much'? Somehow, in all this, I forget that I am part of it too.

Sometimes we forget to feel the suffering that feels distant. There’s an Urdu quote that says, “Pain remains abstract, until it learns your name.” We may not be causing the war—but we are consuming it, and that is dangerous.

Even in times we now remember for their horrors, life did not stop—it simply adjusted. The Book Thief by Markus Zusak, set in Germany during World War II, shows that what feels unsettling today has always been part of human history. The story follows a girl, Liesel, who discovers the magic of words. She once said, “I have hated the words and I have loved them, and I hope I have made them right.” Even Death, the narrator, admits that he is haunted by humans.

Perhaps the past is not as far behind as we think. Both World War II and today’s conflicts have caused human suffering, displacement, economic crises, destruction, mental trauma, and global tension. We mock the past for the mistakes it made, yet somehow we keep repeating them ourselves.

In the end, almost everyone Liesel loved died. For the world it was just another headline.

“A single death is a tragedy; a million deaths is a statistic.”

Maybe the real distance is not of borders, but of names we never learnt.
