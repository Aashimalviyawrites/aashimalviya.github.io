Numbers, not names

April 2026

Christmas had a way of making the house feel softer—quieter, as though even the walls were waiting for something gentle to happen.

“Mom, Dad, what would Santa Claus give me this time?”

“My child, if you’ve been good the whole year, you might get whatever you ask for,” replied Mrs. Joseph.

“Whatever I ask?”

“Yes.”

Hearing this, Ethan ran to his room.

It’s not something I want this Christmas, he thought as he climbed into bed.

❅

On Christmas morning—

“Merry Christmas, Ethan!”

“Merry Christmas, Daddy. Merry Christmas, Mommy.”

“Merry Christ—”

“Look!” Ethan interrupted, turning toward the Christmas tree. “Father Christmas left presents for us.”

Mr. and Mrs. Joseph exchanged a glance.

How was that possible?

“Let’s see what we’ve got… Wow! Skateboards!” Ethan said excitedly. “Daddy, what did you get?”

Mrs. Joseph unwrapped her gift.

“A camera?” she said in surprise.

Mr. Joseph opened his — the latest book by his favourite author.

Both parents turned toward the ten-year-old boy, who then revealed that he had bought the presents with his pocket money.

It was Ethan who broke the silence.

“I know Father Christmas doesn’t exist. It’s the two of you, isn’t it?”

A slight smile appeared on Mrs. Joseph’s face.

“I realised,” Ethan continued, “there is nobody to be your Father Christmas. So I decided to take up the role.”

“And how did you know exactly what we wanted?” asked his father.

“Come on, Dad. I’m your son. I know you the same way you know everything about me.”

“We are a family,” Mrs. Joseph said softly, “and that’s our strength.”

“May it always remain so,” Mr. Joseph said, hugging his son.
